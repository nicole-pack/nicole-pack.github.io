Better Coder

I decided I needed to create my own website when I was applying for jobs and had felt I had nothing to show for my efforts. I had contributed to a moderately impressive project my senior year in college, but not much of it that I could call my own. I had been taking free coding classes online, but didn't find the results very compelling. I felt a drive to code and learn and create new things! But alas, I found myself in the pitfall between beginner and developer.
So, if I was going to have a (in my opinion) mediocre resume, I could most definitely build a website to display my resume in a little bit more exciting way. It could also be a platform to display my finished projects (when I finish them) and blog about my progress.
To begin, I went through the Codecademy HTML and CSS courses and formed a rudimentary "website." This initial site, however, didn't even have a CSS page linked to it. I had become so discouraged at the "simplicity" of the introductory course, that I set the script aside for five months before remembering how excited I had been to do it in the first place and how worth while it is to have the opportunity and ability to brand myself.
So I jumped into it, purposefully hodge-podging my way through a wire-frame set up. After three days, I ended up with a jumble of text elements, placeholders, and piles of nested
's with barebones CSS on a page that honestly didn't look much different from the five-month-old original. While it was fun to observe the little progress I had made in those three days, I realized I could no longer shun the world of tutorials.
The Mozilla Developer Network web docs was the site I chose because I felt I could take it as an authority. The MDN does a good job of explaining the essentials in detail for the HTML tutorial. I found it easy to skim the things I remembered from the Codecademy introduction. I was thankful for their insight on what is common practice for web development as well as considering all users in the explaination of certain tools. Throughout, they speak of the possible downfalls that lazy writing can cause for the visually impaired. While this should be expected (to know your user and teach to cater to them), I was thankful for the humbling experience I got when I realized how terrible my methodology in coding had been this whole time.
From the moment I began coding, I was thrilled to acheive the expected result. When coming up with my own ideas and solutions to problems, the scope of the result was based on what I wanted or needed to happen. Certainly, I did think up ways these ideas would be helpful to others, but they revolved around the way I experienced my code. The casual yet important discussion of writing code that is usable and useful to more than just the majority of people helped me increase in my passion for coding and the work and maintainance it requires! It's not about just finding the right answer, but about creating solutions for everyone who comes into contact with your code.
So for now, I'm still not the greatest web developer. Not by a long shot. But I am a better coder and developer in a most unexpected way. I thought I understood why I love coding so much, but the vast world of computer science has me burning to learn and understand more. Forever a student, I guess. However, it's not such a bad place to be.
